Stop-and-frisk is a controversial police practice that can include observing, stopping, interrogating, frisking, or searching an individual. The New York City, Philadelphia, and Los Angeles police departments have cooperated with court-mandated releases of datasets detailing stop-and-frisk occurrences. Analyses have revealed that stop-and-frisk is racially discriminatory and that it does not lower crime rates, which led to an increase in accountability and a dramatic reduction in the practice with minimal impact on crime rates.

In 2014, the Boston Police department released a dataset of stops-and-frisks that took place between 2007-2010. The ACLU of Massachusetts reported that these police actions followed a familiar pattern: that they were disproportionately targeting Black men. Police Commissioner William Evans publicly responded to the ACLU report in July of 2015, saying that “police officers were not motivated by racial bias.” He further stated that the BPD will revise the stop-and-frisk guidelines to prohibit stops based on race, as part of “a new Bias-Free Policing Policy, which forbids policing based on characteristics such as race and gender.”

The BPD has pledged to regularly release its data for public review, and in January of 2016 released stop-and-frisk data for the years 2011-2015. Data spanning the time after Bias-Free Policing Policy was announced (summer 2015 to present) are currently unavailable. The BPD stated in a letter to the ACLU that the data is “maintained in a different format” and that additional review is needed.
